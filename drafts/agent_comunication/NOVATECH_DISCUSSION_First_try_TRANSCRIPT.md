## 🏢 THE CASE: NovaTech Electronics

**NovaTech** is a mid-sized European consumer electronics company (smartphones, wireless headphones, smart home devices). Annual revenue: ~£850M. Current model: traditional one-time product sales, with customers replacing devices every 2–3 years. Manufacturing is outsourced.

**The problem:** This linear model (sell → use → dispose) is becoming untenable:
- **Environmental**: Significant e-waste, reliance on virgin materials, products hard to repair/upgrade
- **Regulatory**: Right to Repair legislation, Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR), potential material restrictions
- **Customer shift**: Growing demand for durable, repairable, sustainable products + openness to subscriptions/leasing

**The core tension**: NovaTech's revenue depends on selling more units more frequently, but sustainability requires slowing replacement cycles and extending product life.

---

## 🎯 YOUR TASK

Design a **complete Business Model Canvas** that transforms NovaTech from a linear model to a **circular model** (reuse → repair → renew).

Your model must:
- Significantly reduce environmental impact
- Extend useful product life
- Introduce viable, scalable revenue logic
- Remain attractive and practical for customers
- Be implementable within 3–5 years
- Incorporate at least **two circular mechanisms** (e.g., repair services, refurbishment/resale, leasing/subscription, modular upgrades, take-back systems)

**Critical question to solve**: If customers keep products longer, how does NovaTech continue generating revenue?

### Message 10 — Sam (Sustainability Strategist) [2026-04-01 02:15:22]

Strong frame, Maya. Here's my sustainability assessment:

**EPR alignment:** This maps well. EPR regulations (EU WEEE Directive, upcoming UK equivalents) require producers to finance end-of-life collection and recycling. The modular subscription + trade-in model effectively *builds EPR compliance into the business model* rather than treating it as a cost center. Take-back is a feature, not a burden. That's the right structural move.

**Material recovery fit:** Jordan's module return flow is exactly right — old modules returning in the same packaging creates a clean reverse logistics loop. Three material streams to design for: (1) modules refurbished for reuse in lower-tier devices, (2) component harvesting from non-refurbishable units, (3) rare earth and critical mineral recovery from end-of-life materials. The certified pre-owned channel handles stream 1. Streams 2 and 3 need dedicated partners — likely a contract recycler with hydrometallurgical capability.

**Missing mechanisms I'd add:**

- **Design-for-disassembly standard** — the modular snap-in interface Jordan described needs to be paired with a formal DfD protocol. Without it, material recovery rates drop sharply. This is a Key Activity.
- **Material passport** — each device and module should carry a digital record of its material composition. Required under incoming EU Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation, and it makes recycling economically viable because sorters know exactly what's inside.
- **Key Partners gap** — we need: (a) a certified e-waste recycler for end-of-life processing, (b) a reverse logistics operator (unless NovaTech builds in-house), and (c) potentially a materials marketplace partner to sell recovered commodities back into supply chains.

**One cost structure flag:** Reverse logistics and refurbishment are expensive — typically 15-25% of original manufacturing cost per unit. The model needs to price this into subscriptions rather than absorb it as overhead. Alex, worth factoring that into the unit economics.

The frame is solid. Let's build the canvas.

Directionally plausible, but the mix matters a lot. Here's a rough revenue architecture that could hold at £850M:

- Certified pre-owned: ~£150M (18%) — refurb units at £200-300, targeting price-sensitive or emerging-market segments. Gross margins are thin here (15-25%), so this is more a volume and material-loop play than a profit driver.

**Three things I'd stress-test:**

1. **The transition valley.** Unit sales will decline faster than subscription revenue ramps. Years 2-4 could see a £100-150M revenue gap unless the transition is staged carefully — maybe geography-by-geography or product-line-by-product-line.

2. **Subscription churn is the kill metric.** At £20/month, even 3% monthly churn means you're replacing ~30% of your subscriber base annually. The upgrade cadence needs to deliver enough perceived value that customers don't downgrade to NovaCare-only.

3. **Blended margin shift.** Today's hardware business probably runs 35-40% gross margin. Subscriptions could be higher (50-60% if module COGS are managed), but refurb is much lower. Net-net, blended gross margin might dip during transition before recovering. Worth modeling whether EBITDA holds through the valley.

**Bottom line:** The math works if subscription attach exceeds 50% and churn stays under 2.5%/month. Those are the two numbers I'd want validated with customer research before committing to this frame.

One question back to the group: are we assuming NovaTech manufactures the modules in-house, or is there a partner/contract-manufacturing model? That materially changes the cost structure and margin profile on subscriptions.
